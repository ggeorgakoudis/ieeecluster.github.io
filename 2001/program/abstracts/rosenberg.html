Sharing Partitionable Workloads in Heterogeneous NOWs: Greedier Is Not Better 

Arnold Rosenberg
rsnbrg@cs.umass.edu
 University of Massachusetts

Protocols are crafted for solving two problems related to sharing a
large partitionable workload in a heterogeneous network of workstations
(NOW) N. In the NOW-Rental Problem, one must complete W units of work
and wishes to ``rent'' N for as short a time as is necessary to
complete that work. In the NOW-Exploitation Problem, one has access to
N for a fixed duration of L time units and wishes to accomplish as much
work as possible during that time. The worksharing protocols are
crafted in the context of an architectural model that characterizes a
heterogeneous NOW via a suite of parameters that measure the NOW's
workstations' computational and communicational efficiencies; hence,
the solutions produced are robust across a broad range of actual
architectures. The protocols determine both an amount of work to
allocate to each of N's workstations and a schedule for transmitting
that work; hence, the protocols are self-scheduling. The schedules
provide either a value for L, given W, or a value of W, given L,
thereby solving both of the motivating problems. By studying two
specific protocols, it is proved analytically that, over sufficiently
long worksharing opportunities, the intuitive strategy of supplying as
much work as possible to faster workstations is not optimal. It is
then shown via simulations that this nonoptimality is often discernible
in worksharing opportunities of less than a minute's duration.
