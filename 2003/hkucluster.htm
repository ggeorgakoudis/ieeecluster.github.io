The University of Hong Kong (HKU)

HKU Systems Research Group:

The Systems Research Group (SRG) led by Dr. Francis Lau and Dr. C.L. Wang at the Department of Computer Science and Information Systems aims at designing and building real software that run on real hardware to solve real problems, with main research focus on distributed and parallel computing. Over the years, SRG has constructed a number of cluster platforms for supporting systems research and fulfilling the increasing demand on high-performance computing facilities in both CSIS and academic departments in science and engineering disciplines.


Figure 1: Various PC clusters built in SRG,
interconnected by a Gigabit-Ethernet network

In 2002, SRG built the Gideon 300 Cluster. The cluster consists of 300 Intel-based PCs interconnected by two Fast-Ethernet networks. The announcement of the TOP500 supercomputing list (November 2002) has placed Gideon in position 175 based on a measured Linpack performance reaching 355.5 Gflop/s. Apart from supporting SRG's research projects, Gideon has also become a key component of the HKU-CSIS grid point, which is a member of the Asia-Pacific Grid (ApGrid), China National Grid (CNGrid), and the Hong Kong Grid (HKGrid).

    High-Performance Computing Lab.:

    The High-Performance Computing Lab. at the EEE Department, established in 1996 by Prof. Kai Hwang, is now managed by Dr. Ricky Kwok. (http://www.eee.hku.hk/computer/index.html) Two high-end servers (SGI PowerChallenge and Sun Enterprise 6000), together with twenty-one workstations form the ATM-based Pearl Cluster. The Power Challenge contains 8 64-bit MIPS R10000 processors, and its peak performance is 3.2 Gflop/s. The Sun Enterprise contains 8 UltlraSparc II processors. Currently, research projects in the areas of real-time multimedia scheduling algorithm and high-speed video compression techniques are conducted.

    HKU Computer Center:

    Before 1993, the Computer Center (CC) of the University of Hong Kong provided central computing facilities for supporting teaching and research in the University through its VAX 6420 VMS and several UNIX compute-servers. However, many research programs that ran on those systems were computation-intensive. In order to manage the great demand for computer power, HKU CC purchased the IBM 9076 Scalable POWERparallel 1 (SP1) system, which have eight nodes in mid-1993. The theoretical peak performance is up to 1 GFLOPS. After proving the SP1 system is reliable, stable and suitable for both serial and parallel programs, the SP1 was further upgraded to a 48-node SP2 system in December 1998. Each of the nodes consists of a 160MHz IBM P2SC processor, with the theoretical peak performance is 640 MFLOPS per processor, and aggregate peak performance is 30.72 GFLOPS. However, its duty has been taken over by the high-performance computing cluster since 2001 and it has retired by the end of July 2003.


    Figure 5 HKU SP2 http://www.hku.hk/cc/sp2/

    The CC's SP2 offers a general parallel computing platform for teaching and research. Various computation-intensive research projects are conducted by many different departments in HKU, including, physics, civil and construction engineering, mechanical engineering and electrical and electronic engineering. The projects include: Data Mining and Knowledge Discovery, Parallel Image Processing, Mobile Communication (e.g. reliability analysis and maintenance policy of radiators for a large fleet of buses), Parallelization of the H.261 Video Coding Algorithm, Fluid dynamic simulation, Environmental Science (e.g. parallel computation of atmospheric pollutant dispersion), Mathematical Model (e.g. turbulence and dispersion studies using a three-dimensional second-order closure Eulerian mode), Parallel Finite Element Method, Parallel Genetic Algorithm, Mathematical and Numerical Libraries, etc.

    Moreover, every year, there are 70 to 140 graduate students from Computer Science Department use SP2 to practice their parallel programming skill through the course Fundamentals of Parallel Computing taught by Dr. C.L. Wang.

    With the continuous reduction in cost and increase in processing power of PCs, the Computer Centre started a pilot implementation of the Beowulf cluster of Linux systems three years before, as an aim to take over the high performance computing (HPC) services provided by SP2. An 8-node 2-way SMP cluster with Gigabit Ethernet connection was built in Feb. 2001. Each node consists of dual Intel Pentium-III 800 MHz CPU, 1 GB RAM, and 30 GB hard disk, which are interconnected by a 12-port Gigabit Ethernet switch.

    The second, also bigger PC cluster from Compaq was set up in 2001 summer. The cluster comprises of 1 master node and 32 compute (slave) nodes each consisting of two 1 GHz Intel Pentium III processors, 2 GB of RAM and 18 GB of local hard disk. A 252 GB RAID 5 disk is also attached to the master node. These processor nodes are interconnected by a 3Com SuperStack III switch 4300 48 port fast Ethernet switch to support interprocesses communications. This cluster (HKUHPCC) has an aggregate peak performance of 76.8 GFLOPS.

    With the growth trend amongst University members in using parallel computing techniques for solving computation-intense problems in cutting-edge research projects, the loading to the HKUHPCC system is sustained in a very high level for the past year. In this consideration, a much larger cluster has been ordered and will be installed later this year. The system will consist of 128 dual-Xeon processor nodes, with a total GFLOPS rating of more than 1200. The CPU speed will be more than double of the existing 1 GHz Pentium III. This new powerful computer cluster will further enhance the University ability to engage in innovative, high-impact and leading-edge research within and across disciplines.

    The Computer Centre is also currently working on the Grid development area. Recently, CC has successful ported a user s parallelized program to execute on nodes spanning across Hong Kong and Japan. More work will be carried out in this Grid computing project aiming at to integrate multiple servers, storage systems, databases, applications and human resources all over the network.
